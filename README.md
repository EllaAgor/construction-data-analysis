# Great Britain Construction Sector Analysis
Identifying market trends, risks, and delivery capacity based on official ONS construction data

# Executive Summary 
Using Excel and Power BI, I analysed data on construction output, new orders, and firm workforce structure across Great Britain’s construction sector between 1998 and 2024. The analysis highlights that construction activity is primarily driven by private‑sector demand and is highly sensitive to market cycles, with commercial construction forming the core of long‑run output. 

While headline sector categories suggest stability, deeper analysis reveals substantial variation in underlying drivers across sectors. Capacity is concentrated among a relatively small number of large‑workforce firms. 
These findings have implications for early‑stage project planning in an environment characterised by cost inflation, specialist labour constraints, and increasing exposure to delivery risk.


# The Business Problem
Construction work in Great Britain faces heightened uncertainty due to volatile demand conditions, concentrated delivery capacity, and cost pressure arising from inflation and specialist labour constraints. Market‑level construction output indicates activity levels but does not, on its own, reveal where demand is concentrated, how sector priorities differ, or whether the industry has sufficient capacity to deliver work efficiently as demand rises. 

# The Business Questions
- How has construction demand evolved, and which sectors drive long‑run output in Great Britain?
- Do headline sector categories mask different underlying drivers across public and private clients?
- Is construction delivery capacity aligned with demand growth, or is output dependent on a limited subset of firms?

# Key Insights
# Market structure and cyclicality
Private‑sector activity has consistently driven long‑run construction output in Great Britain, exceeding public-sector output over the 1998–2024 period.
- Private sector output dominates overall volumes
- Private output is more volatile across market cycles, with sharper contractions during downturns

# Sector composition masks different demand drivers
Commercial construction represents the largest share of output across both public and private sectors, but is driven by fundamentally different asset types.
- Private commercial output is predominantly office‑led
- Public commercial output is concentrated in education and schools’ delivery

# Structural reallocation of investment within industrial and infrastructure activity
Industrial and infrastructure construction activity has undergone sustained change over the analysis period, reflecting shifting public and private investment priorities.
- Shifts in priority toward warehouses in the private sector, alongside reduced public investment in oil, steel, and coal new work
- Sewerage‑related infrastructure dominated activity up to circa 2014
- Electricity‑focused infrastructure has increased over the past decade
- The private‑sector share of infrastructure output has grown since the early 2000s 
 
# Delivery capacity is concentrated despite a fragmented firm base
Although the construction industry comprises many small firms, delivery capacity and output are concentrated among firms with larger workforces.
- Firm counts are heavily skewed toward small workforce bands
- Output is disproportionately delivered by large‑workforce firms
- Delivery capacity is functionally concentrated by trade and responsibility
  
# Recommendations
- Focus early‑stage advisory effort where risk is structurally concentrated

Areas like private commercial office development are exposed to combined demand volatility, capacity concentration, and cost pressure.
- Engage delivery capability earlier in the planning process

Earlier contractor involvement helps surface labour availability, sequencing constraints, and pricing risk before scope and budget are fixed.
- Strengthen early scope definition and timing sensitivity

A clearer definition at the feasibility stage and awareness of market‑cycle conditions improve cost certainty and reduce exposure to inflation and specialist labour constraints.

# Methodology 
The analysis draws on publicly available data published by the Office for National Statistics (ONS) within the Construction statistics annual tables for Great Britain. The dataset covers the period 1998–2024, with the most recent release issued on 19 February 2026. Data was extracted and analysed using Excel for structuring and Power BI for modelling, aggregation, and visual analysis. 
URL: [Construction statistics annual tables - Office for National Statistics](https://www.ons.gov.uk/businessindustryandtrade/constructionindustry/datasets/constructionstatisticsannualtables)

# Data definitions and assumptions
Construction output represents the value of work carried out within the period and is used as an indicator of construction activity rather than firm‑level revenue.
Repair and maintenance (R&M) refers to work undertaken to maintain or refurbish existing assets and is analysed separately from new construction activity where data availability permits.
Insights are derived from observed trends and relationships within the published data.
